What is El Niño — and why should you care?

El Niño is an abnormal warming of the Pacific Ocean near the equator. It happens every 2–7 years and lasts 9–12 months. For India, it matters because the Indian monsoon’s engine runs on a temperature difference between the Indian Ocean and the Pacific. When the Pacific warms (El Niño), that engine weakens — moisture-laden winds lose strength, and India gets less rain. Historically, 6 of India’s 7 worst droughts since 1950 occurred during El Niño years.

Macro Impact

Agriculture is 15% of GDP but employs 45% of the workforce. A 10% rainfall deficit can shave 0.3–0.5% off GDP growth, spike food inflation by 2–4%, and force RBI to hold rates longer — slowing the entire economy.

Industry Impact

Water-intensive sectors (sugar, textiles, power) face supply squeezes. FMCG companies lose rural demand. Conversely, water infrastructure, packaged food, and thermal power benefit from the scarcity.

Stock Market Impact

Monsoon-sensitive stocks can swing 15–30% in excess returns based on rainfall. Rural-facing companies (tractors, two-wheelers, fertilizers) underperform; defensive and export-oriented names outperform.

Personal Impact

Higher grocery bills (vegetables, pulses, cereals), more expensive electricity (AC usage + lower hydro supply), and potential water restrictions in cities that depend on rain-fed reservoirs.
